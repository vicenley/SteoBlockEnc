\begin{abstract}
	Quantum signal processing (QSP) constructs polynomial
	transformations of a quantum operator's eigenvalues, but the
	signal and its output are fundamentally constrained to lie in
	the interval $[-1,1]$.  We show that this boundedness
	constraint can be lifted without modifying the quantum
	algorithm, by changing only the classical encoding and decoding
	interface.  Stereographic projection compresses an unbounded
	real parameter onto the Bloch sphere, placing it within the QSP
	domain, while Pauli-measurement decoding recovers the
	\emph{ratio} of the two output-state components---an unbounded
	rational function of the original parameter, even though the
	internal polynomials remain bounded.  A simple basis change
	shows that the stereographic and standard QSP signal operators
	are unitarily equivalent, so all existing phase-finding
	algorithms and convergence results apply directly.  The
	base-case functions are identified with Boyd's rational
	Chebyshev functions, connecting QSP to classical approximation
	theory on unbounded domains.  We formulate a stereographic
	block encoding for operators with unbounded spectra, give
	explicit circuit constructions, and show that the coherent
	normalization overhead of standard QSP is replaced by a
	polynomial sampling cost that grows with the eigenvalue
	magnitude.
\end{abstract}
